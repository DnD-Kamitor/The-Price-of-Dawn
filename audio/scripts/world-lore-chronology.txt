Varenholders count years from the Night of the Ritual. Everything before that is "Before" and marked with a B. Everything after is numbered—Year Zero for the ritual itself, Year One for the first winter without sunrise, Year Fifty for now. It is not an academic distinction; people introduce themselves with the year they were born and everyone silently does the math against the Night.

The founding era, roughly two centuries Before, reads like mythology now: eight merchant families build a fortified crossing, the Reach Roads are formalized, guilds take root, and thirty years later the treaty that cements Varenhold's neutrality is signed in the Amber Hall. The golden era follows—amber lantern exports, the Spire expanding, the population peaking at sixty thousand. Archmagister Corven publishes his solar amplification theory in the "Ritual Era" entries and a decade later the Ashring is built to house the spell that will break the world.

Year Zero records the failure. The timeline from Year One to Year Thirty chronicles the dawning realization that the twilight is permanent: crop losses, the rise of the Dawnhalls, the first wave of emigration, the naming of the grey sickness, the Dawnborn manifesting abilities at age ten. The Compact Era shows the city's pivots—credit writs, the Restorers forming, the Desperate Winter where food stores fall to fifteen percent, Lira Anwick's compound keeping people alive. The quiet decades that follow are only quiet because the Dawnhalls and the Dawnborn make them so.

Recent entries are shorter, written with the sharp strokes of crisis notes: Chancellor Mira's election, Brother Edoran's partial revelations, the Reckoning's founding, the grey sickness curve bending upward again, trade partners pulling away. The final line—Year Fifty: campaign begins—is less a date and more an invitation. Someone has to write Year Fifty-One.
