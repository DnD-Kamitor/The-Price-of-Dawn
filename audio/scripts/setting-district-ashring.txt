Walk into the Ashring quarter and the city narrows around you. Pre-twilight stone buildings lean toward each other across alleys that still remember sunlight, but the plaza at the center is stripped bare so you cannot avoid the scorch marks. Thirty feet of stone that refuses to heal sits in perfect, impossible symmetry, and every footstep sounds different when you cross its edge. The air tastes metallic, like lightning just struck and never stopped ringing. Twice a day the Ashring bell tolls and the sound hangs in the plaza longer than it should, as if the stone itself is echoing back.

People move quietly here. Meva the junior archivist locks the secondary Archive with an armful of pre-twilight maps she is not supposed to have. Children dare each other into the scorch circle, giggling until silence catches their throats. Edoran sits alone on the steps watching the center, counting how long he can bear the smell of candle tallow and old dust. A street musician plays a lanternhalt for the fifth time because the fourth left half the crowd crying. The Ashring quarter is not fashionable. It is a living memorial that never decided whether it wants to heal.
