It was hot. That's the thing nobody writes down in the history books because it seems too small, but the people who were there say it first. A late-summer heat with no wind made the stone walls feel warm even after dark. The market smells were stronger than usual—preserved fish, lamp oil, the sweetness of the amber-lantern workshops at full production in the Dawnhalls district. Shadows everywhere. Long ones in the morning, short ones at noon, long again by afternoon, the specific way a shadow tells you where the sun is by its direction and angle. Nobody thought about this. Why would they?

Children played in the plaza near the Ashring. The ritual stones had been installed three years before and were still new enough that parents were careful about letting kids climb on them. The Spire had put up signs asking people not to touch the Primer Stones, and most people respected this in the way you respect a sign when you're an adult. The children didn't respect it. Sera Voss was born at 11:47 that night. She weighed seven pounds.

The amber-lantern workshops ran a full shift. There was a concert in the Amber Hall at seven—the conductor introduced a new work composed for a very specific calibration of warm light. The hall sold out months in advance. Forty-two people stood outside waiting for returns that didn't come. Most of them went home. A few stayed and watched the light from the windows and listened through the walls. The Archive closed at five. Theron Waide, twenty-two years old and two months into his position as Archive Assistant, was the last person to lock the lower floors. He took the long route to the exit because he liked the smell of the old paper in the east stacks. He was outside by five thirty, in the heat, walking home to the Spire Quarter, thinking about dinner. At ten p.m., the Archmagister lit the first of the Primer Stones.

Five people remember that night in their own words. Nessa, then thirty and now eighty, keeping the Dawnhall memorial room, says the light from the Ashring was visible from her window. Blue-white at first, then amber, then a color she doesn't have a word for. The ground felt strange, like the city was breathing differently. Then it stopped. The next morning there was no sun. Her neighbor knocked on the door and said, "Something's wrong." She answered that she knew.

Cova Drell, forty-five then and working the docks, says thousands of people watched the ritual. Even from the back of the crowd he could feel what the Archmagister was doing. Then something tore. The air tore. All the babies in the Lowmark started crying at once. All of them. He had never heard anything like it. He didn't sleep until the second day.

Scholar Innis, sixty at the time and stationed at the Spire, recorded the field readings. Everything matched Corven's predicted model up through Step Nine. Then every instrument pegged to maximum and held there. The charts still exist. The numbers on them are impossible under any theory Innis understood, and he spent thirty years trying.

Hessa, nineteen and an Auris temple novice, says the High Priest was praying when the ritual began. He stopped mid-word, because everyone in the sanctuary felt something—not pain, something stranger, as if the air itself had decided. He turned and said, "Auris is moving." Hessa has wondered for fifty years what direction the god chose.

Aldric Stone, who was born during the backlash, tells the story the way his mother told it. She went into labor the moment the Ashring light changed. He was delivered facing the wrong direction, which apparently meant something in old Varenholder midwifery tradition. He glowed faintly for three days. His mother thought that was beautiful. She never stopped thinking it was beautiful.
