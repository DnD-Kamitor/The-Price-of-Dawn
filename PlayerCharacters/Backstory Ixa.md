Subject: IX Devision: A.
The first thing IX-A or, 9-A ever learned was how to hold still.
Because if she moved, they started over.
“Again.”
The word cracked across the chamber like a whip.
IX-A knelt on the iron ring burned into the floor, small hands spread on her thighs, back straight because they had made it straight, ears lifted because drooping earned correction, sandstone-yellow tail wrapped around her ankles so it would not twitch where the instructors could see.
The chamber was too bright. It was always too bright in the nursery cells. White alchemical lanterns glared behind thick crystal panes in the ceiling, humming faintly with trapped light. Copper veins ran through the stone walls, pulsing with dull violet when the warding arrays cycled. The air smelled of antiseptic herbs, hot metal, old blood scrubbed badly out of grout, and the sweet chemical sting of dream-ink.
She was six.
There was a line of blood running from her left nostril to her lip.
“Again,” said Instructor Meret.
Meret wore gray. They all wore gray at this level, as if grief itself had been stitched into cloth and wrapped around their bones. She was an elf with a face so severe it looked carved rather than grown, and she held the correction rod loosely at her side like she didn’t need it to make children afraid.
IX-A stared at the iron tray in front of her.
On it rested five pebbles.
Simple, round and black.
“Lift one.”
IX-A swallowed, her head was already hurting. She could feel the pressure behind her eyes, the one that came before the splitting ache, before the hot trickle from her nose, before the room started to tilt. She knew if she failed enough times they would not let her sleep. If she failed enough after that, they would call a physician. If a physician came, there would be needles. If there were needles, there would be straps. If there were straps—
“IX-A.”
Meret never raised her voice. She didn’t need to. The number on her tongue was enough.
Meret’s mouth tightened. “Lift. The. Stone.”
IX-A fixed her eyes on the pebble.
She had been told a hundred different ways how to do this. Thought is shape. Thought is force. Thought is pressure. Thought is intent. Thought is obedience. Thought is the hand behind the hand. Thought is sharper than steel. Thought can become steel.
Mostly, thought hurt.
She reached.
There was a strange place inside her that the others kept pulling at, as if there was a hidden tendon in her mind that, when plucked, made the world answer. Finding it felt like sticking her hand into freezing water. Using it felt like trying to breathe through a broken rib.
The pebble trembled.
Lifted.
A finger’s width.
Then dropped.
Meret crossed the distance between them in two silent steps and struck IX-A hard across the shoulder with the rod. Not enough to break bone. Meret never lost control that way. Just enough to make the child gasp and fold sideways.
“Again.”
Tears sprang to IX-A’s eyes.
She bit the inside of her cheek until she tasted blood. Crying made them take notes. Notes meant evaluations. Evaluations meant the physicians. It was better to bleed than to be interesting.
The pebble shook again.
Lifted, then held.
Meret leaned in.
“That is barely lifting,” she said softly.
The rod snapped down across IX-A’s knuckles.
Pain burst white.
The pebble clattered.
“I am trying,” the little tabaxi blurted before she could stop herself.
Meret backhanded her so hard her vision smeared.
“You do not speak unless addressed.”
The room rang. IX-A stared at the floor, ears flattened so hard they hurt, and saw three drops of her own blood spatter between the iron rings.
One.
Two.
Three.
She counted them because counting made the pain into numbers, and numbers were easier than pain.
“Again,” Meret said.
So IX-A sat up. Put her hands back in place. Tucked her tail around herself tighter and reached once more.
She learned quickly in the Crucible that there was no bottom to an “again.”
The Halcyon Crucible had many levels and many names.
Above, in polished documents and speeches delivered to nobles, military councils, and frightened kings, it was called the Halcyon Initiative for Arcane Stability and Peacekeeping. A civilizing force. A bulwark against war. A magitechnical wonder built from the bones of old empires and the brilliance of modern minds.
Below, where the children lived, nobody used the long name.
The staff called it the Crucible when they were being efficient.
The guards called it the Pit when they were being cruel.
The older subjects called it Hell when they were sure no one could hear.
It lay beneath the ruins of a dead city, carved into ancient catacombs, cisterns, crypt-chapels, collapsed royal vaults, and something older still beneath all of that. The Crucible had not been built so much as grown. Every decade they cut deeper. Every decade they widened their cells, their forges, their operating halls, their barracks, their testing arenas, their crematoria.
Its corridors were lined with black stone veined in copper and rune-silver. Glass tubes carried alchemical fluids through the walls like illuminated arteries. Lifts groaned between levels. Security doors sealed with pressure glyphs and blood-key sigils. The observation galleries were made of mirrored crystal so the children could feel watched without always being sure from where.
There were sectors for study. Sectors for combat. Sectors for punishment.
There were nursery wards for the youngest subjects, though “nursery” in the Crucible meant a room with cots, restraints sized for small bodies, and drains in the floor.
There were the learning halls, where subjects were taught language, mathematics, anatomy, loyalty doctrine, military history, and the proper words for what they were becoming.
Asset.
Conduit.
Vessel.
Yield.
Correction.
Continuation.
There were the physician’s theaters, where pain was measured in doses and logged in ledgers.
There were cremation furnaces at the lower levels, because waste needed processing.
There were the tattoo vaults, where ash was mixed with alchemical binders and pressed under skin to “preserve psionic resonance.”
There were records dating back decades, maybe longer, of children taken from villages, purchased from orphanages, bred in-house, traded through desperate families, donated by zealots, stolen in border raids, or born from mothers who never got to keep them.
And then there were the divisions.
A for the Soulknives.
B for the Astrals.
C for the Warriors.
IX-A would not learn that for years.
When she was young, all she knew was Sector A-Black, and the nineteen other children within it.
The children did not meet all at once.
That would have made it too easy to love one another.
The Crucible understood that bonds could make rebellions. So bonds were starved, limited and rationed.
Meals were taken in partitioned stalls separated by iron slats. Lessons were staggered. Bedtimes alternated. Punishments were often public, but comfort never was.
The children learned each other the way blind things learn heat.
By footsteps in the hall.
By coughs behind walls.
By the rhythm of another body crying at night and trying not to.
IX-A knew there was a dwarf in cell III because he muttered numbers in his sleep. She knew VII ground his teeth. She knew XI wet the bed when the dream extraction trials were scheduled. She knew there was another tabaxi somewhere because she sometimes heard the soft thump of a tail hitting a wall when the vents went quiet and the world became small enough to hear everything.
The first time she saw IV-A, she thought: pretty.
She did not know that was or what the thought meant.
She only knew that the girl was the first thing in the Crucible that looked like she belonged somewhere else.
They were eight, brought to Balance Hall Two for a paired coordination trial.
A square chamber. Forty feet across. A pit of black water in the middle that smelled wrong, metallic and stagnant and faintly sweet, like rotting flowers drowned in old coins. Narrow iron beams crossed over it in a lattice. Above, pendulum blades swung on shifting intervals, their hooked edges engraved with sigils that glowed when they cut the air. At the far end stood a pair of stone pedestals with weighted spheres on top.
The trial was simple enough for the instructors to find it elegant: cross the beams, retrieve the spheres, return without dropping them, without touching the water, and without being cut open.
Two children had already fallen that morning.
One lost his balance and had hit the beams on the way down.
He had not come back out of the water.
The other was cut open when the ball was too heavy and he did not jump out of the way in time. His blood was still clinging to the axe.
IX-A stood on the starting platform trying not to stare at the blood still shining on one iron rail.
Beside her, another tabaxi rolled her shoulders as if warming up for a game.
She was built longer and narrower than IX-A, with cream-and-brown fur marked by faint stripes and absurdly pale eyes that seemed silver in the chamber light. Her ears had old notches in them. Her mouth had a crookedness to it, as though smiles came easier on one side.
She looked at the pit, then at the blades, then sideways at IX-A.
“You look like you’re going to throw up.”
IX-A blinked.
No one had spoken to her casually before. Most speech in the Crucible was command, correction, or begging.
“I am not going to throw up,” she said.
The other girl’s gaze dropped to the blood drying at the corner of IX-A’s mouth. “Sure.”
The handler behind them barked, “Silence.”
The pale-eyed tabaxi lowered her voice. “I’m IV-A.”
IX-A frowned slightly. “I am IX-A"
Something flickered over IV-A’s face. Surprise, maybe. Or pity. It was gone too quickly to name.
The crystal gong above the platform flashed.
“Go.”
IV-A moved first. Fast. Not wild-fast. Not panicked. She seemed to understand balance the way some children understood hunger, like it lived in the center of her and told her where not to die.
IX-A followed, breath shallow, tail rigid behind her.
First beam. Second.
The blade came from the left.
IV-A dropped instinctively and shouted, “Third swing comes back fast!”
IX-A had taken the iron ball and looked up at exactly the wrong moment as she nearly took an axe across the face. She jerked backward, heel slipping on iron slick with someone else’s blood. The sphere in her arms slid—
No.
Her mind snapped out.
The black stone weight stopped an inch from tumbling into the pit, hovering in the air, shaking violently.
Pain detonated behind her eyes.
She heard herself whimper.
“Heads down!” IV-A shouted.
IX-A dragged the sphere toward herself with invisible force, muscles shaking as if she were hauling it physically. She clutched it to her chest and stumbled forward again.
The blade kissed her ear on the backswing. A hot line opened there. She smelled her own blood.
Then IV-A’s hand was suddenly on her elbow for one impossible half-second, steadying, guiding, pushing.
“Move, IXA!”
No one had ever said the name like that.
Like it was a real thing.
By the time they made it back to the platform, IX-A was gasping. Her nose was bleeding freely now. Her ear hurt. Her hands would not stop shaking.
The handler grunted approval into his slate, but IV-A leaned in while he wasn’t looking and whispered, “You caught it.”
IX-A stared at her.
“The sphere,” IV-A said, as if she were stupid. “You caught it in the air.”
“Oh.” IX-A looked down at the stone in her hands. “Yes.”
“That was impressive.”
The word hit harder than the rod.
Impressive.
Not adequate, not compliant, but impressive.
IX-A didn’t know where to put that feeling in her body. It was too warm. Too big. She hid it by lowering her eyes.
IV-A, still watching her, smiled slightly. “You really don’t know, do you?”
“Know what?”
“That you’re good.”
The handler slammed the butt of his staff into the floor. “Back in line.”
They obeyed.
But that night, when lights-out came and the vents hissed into low sleep-mode, IX-A pressed her face close to the iron grate in her wall and whispered into the dark, “IV-A?”
Silence for so long she thought maybe she’d imagined the whole thing.
Then, faintly, through the vent system:
“Yeah?”
“I caught it.”
A breath of laughter came through the metal.
“I know.”
And because no one in the Crucible had ever sounded pleased to hear her before, IX-A smiled into the dark where no one could mark it down.
A good friendship begins in crumbs.
A crust of bread slipped under a tray.
A warning tapped in code through the wall before an inspection.
A whispered answer during anatomy recitation so the other wouldn’t lose fingers for failure.
IV-A knew how the Crucible worked in ways IX-A didn’t.
Maybe she was just sharper. Maybe she had broken differently.
“Never be the best at everything all at once,” IV-A whispered through the vent one night.
IX-A lay curled on her cot, blanket pulled up to her nose. “Why?”
“Because then they look at you more.”
“They already look at me.”
“Not like they will.”
There was a rustle on the other side, the sound of someone shifting on thin bedding.
“You have to let them think you’re manageable,” IV-A said. “A little strange. A little useful. Never irreplaceable.”
“I am is not replaceable.”
IV-A went quiet.
When she answered, her voice had changed.
“No,” she said softly. “You are. That’s the problem.”
IX-A frowned into the dark. “I do not understand.”
“Good.”
Another night, after sensory deprivation.
“What is sky?” IX-A whispered.
The pause was so long she thought IV-A had fallen asleep.
“Blue,” IV-A said at last.
“That is a color.”
“I know.”
“Then what is sky?”
This time IV-A made an annoyed noise. “It’s... above.”
“Ceiling is above.”
“It isn’t a ceiling.”
“Then why doesn’t it fall?”
“I don’t know!”
The whisper came back sharper, then softened immediately. “Sorry.”
IX-A tucked her aching hands under her cheek. “Have you seen sky?”
“No.”
“Then how do you know?”
More silence.
Then: “Because one of the attendants had a book once. Before they took it away.”
“What was in the book?”
“Pictures.”
“Of sky?”
“And fields. And mountains. And this thing called the sea.”
“What is sea?”
IV-A laughed under her breath, helplessly. “Gods, you are impossible.”
“What is sea?”
“It’s water.”
“There is water here.”
“Not like that.”
“How?”
“Bigger.”
“How big?”
“Bigger than this place.”
IX-A went still.
Her whole world fit in corridors and cells and chambers and pits. Even the biggest halls ended in walls. Bigger than the place meant impossible.
“Nothing is bigger than this place,” she whispered.
“Yes, there is.”
“How do you know?”
IV-A did not answer for a long time. When she did, her voice was quiet enough to nearly vanish in the vents.
“Because if there isn’t, then this is all there is.”
IX-A didn’t understand why the thought made IV-A sound like she was hurting. So she said the only thing she could think of.
“I hope that sky is real.”
Something scraped softly against metal on the other side. A hand, maybe, resting against the vent. IX-A put her own against the grate.
“Me too,” IV-A whispered.
Their fingers could not touch through the iron.
But IX-A fell asleep with her palm there anyway.
The older they grew, the more the Crucible made monsters of them on purpose.
At nine, armed drills began.
At ten, fasting trials and toxin tolerance.
At eleven, dream extraction.
That was the year the physicians started entering their sleeping chambers at night with polished brass carts and careful voices, waking them under low lantern light and saying things like, “No need to be afraid,” while fitting cold silver caps to their heads.
The cap had needles on the inside.
Very fine. Very many.
IX-A learned that if she screamed before they fastened the jaw strap, they stuffed cloth in her mouth and wrote that she was resistant. Resistance lengthened the session.
So she stopped screaming.
The machine that harvested dreams made a sound like wet teeth clicking together.
The room would dim. The silver cap would bite. And then they would ask questions while images were pulled from the back of her head and made visible in glass.
“What do you see when you imagine safety?”
“What shape does fear take?”
“When your psionic field destabilizes, what color does the void become?”
“If you could leave, where would you go?”
IX-A did not know most of the answers.
Sometimes she lied.
“Nowhere,” she said once, when a physician asked where she would go.
He smiled while adjusting a dial. “Good.”
But the machine made liars of children. It reached into the softest parts of them and dragged pictures to the surface anyway.
A patch of warmth.
A hand she had never actually held, only wanted.
Something blue overhead she had built from IV-A’s borrowed descriptions.
A field of silver fish that made no sense because she had never seen fish, but wanted IV-A to be right about them.
The physician took notes the whole time.
When the session ended, blood ran down her neck beneath the collar.
She staggered back to bed and once, only once, found a scrap of cloth pushed under her door.
Not regulation issue. Torn from an undershirt. Clean.
For the blood.
No note. No name.
But the next day IV-A had a fresh rip in the hem of her own sleeve.
IX-A stared at it during line formation.
IV-A didn’t look at her. Just muttered, out of the corner of her mouth, “What?”
IX-A looked down. “Nothing.”
But all day the scrap cloth stayed hidden in her fist, warm from her skin.
At eleven and a half, the tattoos began.
The first time, they took IX-A to Ink Vault Three.
The room was circular and much too warm, filled with braziers burning resin that smelled sweet enough to make her nauseous. The walls were covered floor to ceiling in diagrams of the body, marked not with muscles and organs but with channels of force, anchor points, resonance wells, fracture zones. Metal trays held needles of different lengths. Porcelain bowls contained inks in shades of black, violet, and old rust.
At the center of the room sat a chair with straps.
Children noticed those first, always.
Magister Thorne stood beside it.
Thorne was what the Crucible had instead of a saint. The physicians deferred to him. The instructors hated him but obeyed him. He moved like someone too certain of his own importance to fear becoming a corpse.
He was elven, tall, elegant, all pale hair and clean hands and a voice like silk pulled over a blade.
“IX-A,” he said warmly, which was always worse than anger. “Come.”
IX-A stopped three paces away. “Why?”
Thorne smiled.
A physician behind her drove a needle into the side of her neck.
Her legs went weak immediately.
“I asked a question,” she whispered, horrified at the softness creeping through her body.
“And I indulged you long enough to hear it,” Thorne said.
They strapped her in.
Wrists first. Ankles. Chest. Forehead.
The leather was dark and cracked from use. Someone had scratched little notches into the armrest. Dozens of them.
IX-A stared at those while they pinned her tail gently aside and bared the skin over her collarbone.
“Do you know what ash is?” Thorne asked.
IX-A’s breathing sped up. “Burned things.”
“Very good.”
He lifted a shallow porcelain bowl.
Inside, the ink shimmered faintly violet-gray, too luminous to be ordinary pigment.
“Residual psionic charge can persist after death if properly rendered and bound. Such a waste to bury potential.” He glanced down at her with approving interest. “We do not waste in the Crucible.”
A child’s voice broke from the chair before IX-A could stop it. “Whose is it?”
Thorne dipped the needle.
“A precursor.”
He might as well have said no one.
The first puncture nearly made her black out.
The ink burned under the skin like something alive and furious was trying to claw its way in through the wound. Not heat. Heat would have been simple. This was a crawling fire, moving along nerves, catching in the hollows under bone. She felt every point of the needle as a separate betrayal.
When she bucked, the straps groaned.
“Hold her,” one assistant said.
“I am holding her,” another snapped.
Thorne worked slowly. Deliberately. A branching sigil beneath the clavicle, then lines radiating out over the shoulder, then tiny hooked marks that sat directly over nerve clusters to increase “conduction.”
IX-A screamed until her voice went raw.
By the end she could no longer feel her left hand.
Thorne wiped the blood away with a cloth and stepped back to admire the work on her skin.
“Beautiful,” he said.
IX-A vomited down the front of the chair, but his expression didn’t change.
That night the sigil glowed faintly in the dark.
IX-A clawed at it until her nails filled with blood.
It still healed.
And when it did, she could hold a formed blade twice as long.
The Crucible called that success.
At twelve, they made the children kill one another on purpose.
They had spent years preparing them for it, shaping their instincts so survival and violence braided together too tightly to separate. Pairing stronger subjects against weaker ones. Rewarding betrayal. Punishing mercy. Making children watch what happened to those who hesitated.
The formal announcement came in Observation Hall One.
The twenty subjects of Division A stood barefoot in two rows on black stone polished so smooth it reflected their legs like dark water. Above them, behind mirrored crystal, silhouettes shifted in the galleries. Investors, perhaps. Military officers. Senior staff. People who liked to observe death from seats with armrests.
Director Severin Vale addressed them from a raised platform.
He looked like a man who believed history would thank him.
Human. Tall. Immaculate. One eye flesh, the other an articulated crystal lens set in silver and rune-work, turning faintly as it focused. His uniform was black with violet piping at the seams and the sigil of the Halcyon Initiative over the heart: a circle broken by three upward spears.
“You were not made to live ordinary lives,” he said.
His voice carried effortlessly through the hall.
“You were made to end them.”
No one moved.
No one even breathed too loudly.
“The world above fractures itself over borders, bloodlines, gods, and greed. Kingdoms collapse. Republics rot. Magic runs wild in the hands of the incompetent. We will bring peace. Not by appealing to weakness, but by perfecting strength.”
He looked at them as if he had forged each child himself out of ore and noble intention.
“Division A will produce the Soulknives. You will infiltrate, sever, remove. You will be precision where others are chaos. From your ranks, the superior specimens will advance.”
Specimens.
Not even assets today.
IX-A stood with her hands at her sides and felt IV-A’s knuckle brush hers once, fleeting, unseen.
“There are twenty of you,” Vale said. “There need not remain twenty.”
The words passed through the hall like cold water.
IX-A turned her head before she could stop herself.
IV-A didn’t look at her.
But her jaw had gone hard enough to crack teeth.
The Red Chamber was larger than the others and cleaner.
That was somehow the worst part.
Black floor, copper channels, mirrored walls, no clutter, no blood stains left to dry in corners. The place had been designed for violence that the audience wanted to see clearly.
IX-A stood in one circle. VII-A stood in the other.
He was a dwarf with a shaved head and forearms thick with old bruises. He had once given IX-A half his bread because she’d been shaking too badly after a seizure to hold the spoon. Three weeks later he’d broken III-A’s arm during a trial because the instructors told him to.
In the Crucible, kindness and cruelty lived in the same body because children adapted or died.
The bell rang.
VII-A moved first with both palms thrust out.
The force hit like a wall dropped from nowhere. IX-A threw herself sideways and still caught the edge of it. Pain shot through her ribs. She rolled, came up low, formed two psionic shards, and sent them toward his face.
He blocked one. The other scored his cheek.
Blood welled bright.
He grinned.
That frightened her more than if he’d screamed.
He came at her hard, driving invisible pressure before him in crushing waves. IX-A dodged one, slipped the second, got clipped by the third and hit the ground on one knee, vision flashing.
Above, glass. Notes. Watching.
She was losing.
VII-A saw it too.
He charged.
IX-A looked up and saw not an opponent but the boy who muttered numbers in his sleep. The one who had once hidden a spoonful of mashed root for XI-A because XI-A had been punished with fasting. The one who had laughed exactly twice in four years, both times at something IV-A had whispered.
A child.
Then he raised both hands to cave her chest in.
Something inside IX-A tore.
She did not think a blade into being. The blade was simply there, forming in front of her in a burst of violet light so sharp it hummed. It punched up through the underside of VII-A’s jaw and out through the roof of his mouth.
The sound he made was wet and tiny.
He collapsed over her.
Warm blood spilled down her throat.
The chamber disappeared.
No.
Not disappeared.
Changed.
The color of blood softened into wine-dark paint. The weight of the body became someone lying down. The choking became coughing in sleep. The dead open stare became closed eyes in her mind, because closed eyes were safer.
Hands dragged him off her. Hands touched her. Voices talked over her.
“Excellent penetration.”
“Record the reaction.”
“No hesitation after the stress spike—”
“Look at me, IX-A. Look at me.”
Thorne’s face floated into view.
“What happened?”
IX-A stared past him.
VII-A lay on the black floor. Still. Quiet. Resting.
The room pulsed strangely at the edges.
“Ixa put him to sleep,” she whispered.
One of the assistants drew a sharp breath.
“Cognitive severance.”
“Traumatic dissociation,” said another.
Thorne studied her for a long moment, then smiled the way some men smiled at new weapons.
“No,” he said. “Adaptive mercy.”
He touched the blood at the corner of her mouth with gloved fingers and held it up to the light as if it were beautiful.
“Tell me, Ixa. Do you feel guilty?”
Ixa frowned, confused.
“Why?”
That was the answer he wanted.
After that, the fracture widened.
For four years they fed it.
That was what made the Crucible a horror: it did not merely break children. It learned from the break and optimized around it.
Once Thorne understood that Ixa could kill more efficiently if her mind translated murder into sleep, the staff altered their methods. They reinforced the delusion. Softened certain words around her. Adjusted the post-combat debriefs. Removed bodies before she could see too much. Let her believe, whenever possible, that the stillness after violence was rest.
“Subject is asleep.”
“Escort the sleeper.”
“Prepare the next sleeper for transfer.”
Ixa accepted it because the alternative would have split her mind wide open.
When she woke sweating from dreams of claws in flesh and teeth and screaming, they dosed her.
When she stared too long at the cremation chimneys, they redirected her.
When she asked why some sleepers never came back, a physician once smiled and said, “Different rooms.”
Sometimes, though, the truth leaked through anyway.
A hand hanging off a steel gurney with blood under the nails.
A furnace door opening.
A tray of gray ash still warm.
The smell.
Gods, the smell.
You can clean blood from stone.
You cannot clean burned people from memory.
IV-A was the only thing that made those years survivable.
They were fourteen the first time Ixa realized she looked for IV-A before she looked for exits.
A dangerous thing in the Crucible.
They were lined up in Conditioning Hall after punishment laps. Half the children were limping. One boy had vomited and been ordered to keep running through it. The handlers paced with correction rods between the rows.
Ixa’s breath hurt. Her side had a stitch in it. There was blood matted into the fur behind one knee.
Across the line, IV-A lifted her chin a fraction and widened her eyes theatrically toward the handler behind him.
Ridiculous.
A face that said can you believe this idiot?
Ixa stared.
Then, before she could help herself, a laugh burst out of her. Small. Choked. Wrong place, wrong time.
The hall went dead silent.
The handler turned.
“Who made that sound?”
No one answered.
His gaze landed on Ixa.
Of course it did.
He walked to her slowly, savoring it. “Was it you, IX-A?”
Ixa dropped her eyes. “No, sir.”
He struck her across the mouth.
“Liar.”
Blood filled her mouth instantly.
Out of the corner of her eye, she saw IV-A go perfectly still. Not flinching. Not reacting. But a stillness so absolute it became its own kind of scream.
“Fifteen lashes,” the handler said. “Public.”
Ixa did not cry during the first six.
By seven the skin across her back was open.
By ten her knees gave.
At twelve her vision went gray.
At fifteen she was barely conscious.
The whole cohort was forced to watch.
Later, after lights-out, a whisper came through the vent, so tight with fury it shook.
“I’m sorry.”
Ixa lay on her stomach, raw and feverish, every breath scraping. “Why?”
“Because I did that.”
“No. The rod did that.”
“Ixa—”
“It was funny.”
On the other side of the wall, IV-A made a sound Ixa had never heard from her before. Not laughter. Not anger.
A muffled sob.
Ixa went still.
“IV-A?”
No answer.
“IV-A?”
When the voice came back, it was careful. Strangled around something too large to fit through a vent.
“When we get out,” IV-A whispered, “I’m going to find the first person who ever taught me that laughing was dangerous, and I’m going to feed them their own teeth.”
Ixa, half-delirious with pain, smiled against her blanket.
“You say that a lot.”
“I mean it every time.”
“You are violent.”
“Look who’s talking.”

Ixa didn’t have words for what she felt then. For the way IV-A’s voice could pull her back from the edges of herself. For the way she hoarded scraps of IV-A’s speech in her mind like precious things. For the sick fear that gripped her whenever IV-A was late to training and she had to wait, not knowing if late meant injured or punished or gone.
She had no word for wanting.
Only for need.
And she needed IV-A like some people needed air and gods and a horizon.
At fifteen, the cremation-tattoo cycle changed.
Before, ash had come from failed precursors and older stock, anonymous and clinical.
Then the cohort numbers dropped.
Then the ashes became personal.
The first time they used someone Ixa had known, it was III-A.
The replacement III-A had lasted only two months.
Not the same left-handed human child who’d been brought in after the first III died. Another one after that, then another. Numbers recycled. Bodies didn’t.

A human girl thrown backward off a platform, spine bending wrong on the landing.

No, thought Ixa’s fracture softly. Sleeping.

Then fell.
Sleeping on a bed of red.

Move.

Then folded.

A child.
A sleeper, corrected the fracture.

IV-A did not want to lose her.
The realization moved through her like a lit match dropped into dry grass.
She didn’t know what to call it. It had no shelf in her mind to sit on. So it became heat. Fear. Need. A trembling under her ribs that made breathing hard.

It came out too honest.

For a heartbeat, she looked older than eighteen. Older than anyone should.

“I know.”

The world stopped.
Maybe not the real world. The fake one in Ixa’s head. The manageable one. The one where children only slept and furnaces were just hot rooms and wanting something badly enough didn’t feel like starvation.
Something in that sentence reached in and put a hand around its throat.
“Ixa wants sky with you,” she whispered.
IV-A’s face crumpled.
Just for a second. Just enough to see it.

A reminder. A command.
Do it.

She had to. Because if she waited, Ixa never would have.
The fight between them was brutal because it had to be convincing. Brutal because they knew each other too well to fake it. Every weakness one had, the other had seen. Every pattern they’d trained into each other in the practice halls became a wound waiting to happen.
IV-A cut Ixa across the shoulder. Ixa drove a blast of force into IV-A’s sternum hard enough to send her skidding back across wet stone. IV-A recovered instantly, vaulted a broken column, came down with claws glowing white-violet, and slashed at Ixa from three angles at once.

At one point IV-A pinned Ixa against a cracked pylon, forearm to her throat, breath ragged against her face.

“For this.”

A desperate, shaking press of blood-warm mouth to mouth, as if she had reached the end of language and this was all that was left.

Hard.
