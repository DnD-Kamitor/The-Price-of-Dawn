# The Price of Dawn — Plot Overview

*A campaign design document by Graeme Sherwood / theGreatGM*

---

## The Logline

Ten people are carrying the sun inside them. The city needs it back. Whose choice is that to make?

---

## The World in Thirty Seconds

Varenhold has lived in artificial twilight for fifty years. The Ritual of Eternal Dawn was meant to bring permanent sunlight — instead it failed, killed Archmagister Corven, and left the city in amber stasis. Grey sickness spreads. Food runs short. Three good harvests left, maybe five.

The same night the ritual failed, ten children were born in perfect health under impossible circumstances: the Dawnborn. They have spent fifty years being the city's beloved symbols of hope. They have also been slowly dying alongside everyone else — they just don't know why yet.

**The truth:** the ritual didn't fail. It redirected. The ten anchor-points Corven tried to create in the world found living hosts instead. The Dawnborn *are* the ritual. They've been holding the energy in their bodies for fifty years. To complete the ritual — to bring the sun back — that energy has to be released.

Releasing it means they die.

---

## The Central Dilemma

*Is hope worth chasing when you don't yet know what it costs? And once you know: who gets to decide?*

This is a campaign about consent, sacrifice, and the difference between a city that loves its symbols and a city that respects its people.

Three factions have three answers:

**The Restorers** (Brother Edoran) say: the Dawnborn were always going to die for this. The twilight is killing everyone — better a meaningful death than a slow one. Edoran lost his daughter to grey sickness and organized his grief into a theological position. He is not wrong about the math. He is complicated about the consent.

**The Reckoning** (Commander Harran) say: the Dawnborn don't get a vote. The city's survival takes precedence. This is exactly what it looks like, and Harran knows it.

**The Chancellor** (Mira Ostenveld) says: find an answer that doesn't require me to know the cost. She chose strategic ignorance and she knows it. Players will recognize this — it's the same choice they make in Session 1, when they take the job before asking what the job actually is.

## The Story Arcs

### Main Arc: The Ritual

**Session 1 (L4):** Players investigate the ritual's history without knowing the cost. They meet Sera Voss and the Dawnborn. They fall in love with the hope. This is deliberate design — the dilemma only lands when the hope is real.

**Session 2 (L5):** The cost lands. Archivist Theron Waide has known for eleven years and said nothing — guilt, not malice. The Reckoning escalates into violence. Players now carry the weight.

**Session 3 (L6):** Three alternatives emerge, none of them clean. Players must choose a direction while the city fractures around them.

**Session 4 (L7):** Everything converges at the Ashring ritual site. The Primer Stone reveals a hidden pathway. The Reckoning forces a confrontation. Time runs out.

**Session 5 (L8):** The ritual. Whatever the players chose, they see it through.

---

### Secondary Arcs

**Theron's Arc** — A man who found the truth eleven years ago and couldn't act. His guilt is the slow drip beneath Sessions 1 and 2. His confession is one of the campaign's quietest gut-punches.

**Edoran's Arc** — Grief organized into certainty. The most dangerous kind of antagonist: partly right. Players who engage seriously may find themselves agreeing with him on some things — which makes disagreeing on the central question much harder.

**The Dawnborn Consent Arc** — Ten individual arcs, each different. Sera is already willing. Lira has a three-year-old daughter and is absolutely not. Tomas has done the math and is writing his will. Cormac said yes nine weeks ago and isn't sure he means it anymore. Ysel told Edoran yes months before the players arrived, and she is waiting for someone — anyone — to ask if her consent was genuinely free. Every yes and no is different. None of them are simple.

---

## The Three Alternative Paths (Session 3)

This is where the campaign becomes a real moral problem rather than an investigation:

1. **Isolde's Transfer Method** — Move the anchor energy into constructed vessels. The ritual fires. A 15–20% chance that one or two Dawnborn die anyway. Science instead of hope.

2. **Tomas's Asymmetry** — Five "surge-phase" Dawnborn carry double the load. Five could theoretically complete the ritual, protecting the other five. But it requires identifying which five — and asking them.

3. **The Inversion Pathway** (revealed by the Primer Stone in Session 4) — If all ten Dawnborn freely consent — genuinely, without coercion or pressure — the ritual inverts. The sun returns. No one dies. This is the "good" ending, and it is significantly harder than letting the Reckoning do it, because *every single one* has to choose. Including Lira.

---

## The Five Endings

| Ending | What Happens |
|--------|-------------|
| **A — The Willing Sacrifice** | Consenting Dawnborn complete the ritual. Sun returns. Some die. The moral weight: who truly chose, who was persuaded, who was pressured — and do the players know the difference? |
| **B — The Inversion** | All ten freely consent. Sun returns. No one dies. Requires genuinely earning every Dawnborn's trust, including the ones with reasons to refuse. Possible. Hardest path. |
| **C — The Transfer** | Isolde's method. Sun returns. 15–20% chance one or two Dawnborn still die. Workable, unglamorous, liveable. |
| **D — The Refusal** | Players protect the Dawnborn over the ritual. The sun stays dark. Grey sickness progresses. The city adapts, or doesn't. A valid ending that says Dawnborn lives are worth more than the city's comfort. |
| **E — Cascade Failure** | The Reckoning forces unwilling Dawnborn into the ritual. Cooperative resonance breaks. Uncontrolled discharge. All ten die. The ritual fails. The sun doesn't return. This is what happens when the answer to *"who gets to decide?"* is *"whoever has the most soldiers."* |

## The GM's Job

Make sure the players love the Dawnborn before the cost is revealed. Then make sure they understand that some of those Dawnborn have already decided.

The moral dilemma isn't: *should we do the ritual?*

It's: *we were hired to find an answer, we found one, and now we know what it costs — and we are the only people in this story with enough information to do something real about it.*

The city drifts toward catastrophe with or without the players. The players aren't protagonists because they were hired. They're protagonists because they're the only ones willing to hold all the information at once and make a genuine choice.

Give them real choices. Make the cost real. Let them be the people who did the thing, whatever the thing was.

That's the whole game.
